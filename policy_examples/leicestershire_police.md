# 1. Statement of Purpose

Artificial Intelligence (AI) technologies are increasingly being adopted across policing to enhance the delivery of services. When implemented safely, ethically, and appropriately, AI can significantly improve how we manage and analyse data, and how we communicate with and support the public, staff, third-party partners, and suppliers. 

Leicestershire Police have established this document to provide clear direction on the responsible use of AI technologies. It aims to ensure that all deployments of this technology are subject to robust oversight and risk management processes, in line with legal, ethical, and operational standards. 

The use of AI in the Force can broadly be split into three capability areas: 

- AI supporting organisation effectiveness and efficiency 

- AI supporting operational effectiveness and efficiency 

- AI supporting individual decision making and expediting processes 

This document also outlines the governance process that must be followed prior to the deployment of any AI technology within the Force. It also establishes the key principles that will regulate the use of AI throughout its lifecycle, ensuring transparency, accountability, and alignment with the Force's values and strategic objectives. 

The adoption of AI within the policing community is considered a collaborative effort, with guidance and standards published by a number of sources. The following represent a non-exhaustive list of supplementary material which also should be considered when introducing a new AI related technology into the Force's environment: 

NPCC Cyber Standard - Artificial Intelligence 

- NPCC Cyber Standard - Artificial Intelligence Large Language Models 

NPCC Cyber Standard - Artificial Intelligence Risk Assessment 

NPCC Covenant for Using Artificial Intelligence (AI) in Policing 

NPCC Artificial Intelligence (AI) Strategy 

NPCC AI Play Book 

UK Governments Responsible AI Toolkit 

UK Government Code of Practice for the Cyber Security of AI 

The rapid nature of the development and application of AI is such that this document will be subject to regular review. Due to this, it is important to note that certain applications of the use of AI within the Force, especially if it is found to be being misused or considered to bring the Force into disrepute can be revoked at any time by the Force Senior Information Risk Owner (SIRO), a role held by the Deputy Chief Constable. 

# 2. Scope

The Chief Constable has overall direction and control of Leicestershire Police and is ultimately accountable for all policies and supporting documents of the organisation 

and its actions. Adherence to this force policy will be expected from: all Officers, Special Constables, Police Staff, Police Support Volunteer, Contractors, agency staff and any other individual provided access to the Force's IT environment. 

In accordance with this document, it is the duty of all staff to ensure that any usage of AI is done so in a manner that promotes fairness and avoids bias and continues to align with the Forces values and ethical standards. 

# 3. What is Artificial Intelligence?

There is currently no universally accepted legal definition of Artificial Intelligence (AI). The Alan Turing Institute (2021) acknowledges that AI is often used as an umbrella term encompassing related technologies such as automation, neural networks, and machine learning. For clarity and consistency within policing, Leicestershire Police adopt the following working definitions, aligning with the definitions utilised by the National Police Chiefs' Council (NPCC) and the Police Digital Service. Additionally, practical examples of each definition have been provided to assist the reader. 

- Artificial Intelligence (AI) refers to a machine or system that learns, generalises, or infers meaning from input data, thereby reproducing or surpassing human performance. This includes tasks such as image analysis to detect illegal content or predictive modelling for operational planning. AI may also refer more loosely to systems capable of performing repetitive tasks without human guidance. 

An example of this type of AI would be a system that analyses CCTV footage, to determine if it contains illegal activities such as child sexual abuse, without the need for human intervention to review the footage. 

- Machine Learning (ML) is a subset of AI involving algorithms that improve their performance over time by learning from new data, without being explicitly programmed. ML underpins many modern policing tools, including speech recognition, fraud detection, and behavioural analytics. 

An example of ML is a fraud detection tool that becomes more accurate as it learns from patterns in previous financial crimes. 

- Advanced Data Analytics (ADA) involves the use of specialised techniques and subject matter expertise to extract insights from complex datasets. ADA goes beyond traditional business intelligence and may include methods such as Risk Terrain Modelling, linear regression, and language analytics to support strategic decision-making. 

An example of ADA is a hotspot in policing tool which identifies areas with higher likelihood of crime based on environmental factors, guiding where officers should be deployed. 

There are other related AI terminology, which start to overlap with the above, but are included here for completeness. The above and below are considered the most common, but there are others. The additional definitions are 

- Generative Artificial Intelligence (GAI) refers to AI systems capable of creating new content—such as text, images, or audio—based on patterns learned from training data. These models are increasingly used in investigative and administrative contexts, such as automated report drafting or evidence summarisation. 

- Large Language Models (LLMs) are a subset of GAI trained on vast amounts of text data, enabling them to generate human-like responses and perform tasks such as translation, summarisation, and conversational interaction. Examples include ChatGPT and Google Bard, to summarise lengthy policy documents or assist with drafting emails. 

- Natural Language Processing (NLP) is the branch of AI focused on enabling machines to understand and interpret human language. NLP is used in tools that transcribe interviews, analyse sentiment, and extract key information from documents. 

Overall, the term "AI" has ultimately been adopted as an umbrella term that has encompassed various forms of technology. All users of this framework must be alive to the fact that AI technology is not always visible, especially where it forms part of a background function incorporated into an application or similar tool. 

Please note: Despite any terminology utilised for the naming of a type of an AI, this document is considered to be applicable to ALL types of AI regardless of their application. Should any doubt arise, please contact the IT Service Desk or Information Security team for further advice.

# 4. AI Deployment Types

Leicestershire Police currently deploy Artificial Intelligence (AI) technologies in two primary forms. 

# 4.1 Integrated AI Tools

Integrated AI refers to AI capabilities embedded within the existing platforms and systems used across the Force. These features are often part of commercial or operational software, and its use may not be overtly visible to users. Examples include: 

- Automated redaction tools within document management systems. 

- Facial recognition features embedded in digital evidence platforms. 

- Microsoft's embedding of Co-Pilot within the Microsoft Office Suite of products 

These technologies are increasingly common across public sector services and are adopted to enhance efficiency, accuracy, and data handling practices. All integrated AI deployments are subject to a specific risk assessment process, led by the Information Management department in collaboration with key stakeholders such as IT and Procurement. For AI deployments of this nature, each AI feature is evaluated for: 

- Appropriateness of use within the policing context. 

- Technical Safeguards to protect sensitive Force data. 

- Compliance with legal, ethical, and operational standards. 

It is not the intention of this document to provide a comprehensive list of all integrated AI tools authorised for use in Force, however key examples of Integrated AI tools within the Force include: 

- Nice Investigate - The Force's Digital Evidence Management solution 

GoodSam - A tool for communicating with the public 

- Riven DocDefender - A tool for redacting documents 

These examples provide a practical example of the Force's three capability areas for the use of AI, as each application seeks to introduce efficiency savings in a secure manner. 

# 4.2 Standalone AI Tools

Standalone AI tools refer to applications where the AI model is directly accessible to the user as its own application, rather than as part of another system. These tools are most commonly of the Generative AI type allowing users to upload Force data and interact with the model via prompts—for example, asking the AI to analyse an Excel spreadsheet or summarise a document. 

Like Integrated AI, all standalone AI tools are subject to a specific risk assessment process led by the Information Management team, in collaboration with stakeholders such as IT and Procurement. However, due to their extensive capabilities and potential for uncontrolled data processing, these tools present a heightened risk and are therefore subject to strict regulation within policing, with stakeholders such as the Police Digital Service providing additional guidance to the Force for their safe deployment. 

As of the date of this document, the following represent the only standalone AI applications authorised for use by Leicestershire Police personnel: 

- Microsoft 365 Copilot Chat (Free Version) 

- Microsoft 365 Copilot (Licensed Version) 

No other standalone AI tools may be used within the Force. This includes, but is not limited to, the following prohibited commercial AI tools: 

- Microsoft Copilot (Consumer/Personal versions) 

ChatGPT 

- Deepseek 

Gemini 

- Perplexity 

- Self-hosted, either on-premises or cloud based, LLM's or generative AI tool such as open-source models. 

- AI tools integrated into existing products where they are not explicitly authorised in section 5.1 

- Any other commercially available Large Language Model (LLM) or Generative AI tool 

# 4.3 New AI Tools

Integrated AI tools that are part of a wider product being adopted do not require endorsement by the AI Board, as their procurement route will already include an evaluation of the AI tooling. 

The use of new standalone AI tools must be approved by the Artificial Intelligence Board, alongside any other governance required for that product (such as procurement, information security, information management etc). 

The Board will consider the tools compliance in respect of this policy and any national guidance, alongside the ethics of its use and any steps taken by the tool or intended users to mitigate any identified risks. 

New AI tools will be considered within the three following 'buckets': 

- Prompted AI – Tools that require direct user input, such as CoPilot Chat, to interact with. 

- No-code or low-code AI – Tools that allow automation using AI, such as CoPilot Studio or Power Automate using AI connectors. These tools allow AI to be used within automated workflows, whether or not they require human input at some stage. Workflows are usually designed in a visual interface and require no, or minimal, code. 

- Coded AI – Tools that require custom development to implement, such as a Python script that connects to a database via an API and processes the data through an LLM model. 

The governance process in respect of gaining authorisation for the use of new AI tools is contained within Annex A. The process differs depending on the 'bucket' of tool intended to be used, as the risks associated with each 'bucket' are different.

# 5.1 Intended uses of AI tools at Leicestershire Police

Artificial Intelligence (AI) presents significant opportunities for modern policing. From enhancing operational efficiency and improving decision-making to supporting frontline officers and streamlining administrative tasks, AI technologies can deliver real-world benefits across the Force. Leicestershire Police recognises the value of innovation and is committed to enabling the safe and responsible adoption of AI. 

AI is intended for use in the following types of activities within Leicestershire Police. 

# 1. Productivity and Admin Support

- Draft emails, reports, and meeting notes 

Summarise documents or transcripts 

- Improve clarity and tone of writing 

- Create templates, checklists, or standard replies 

# 2. Data and Analysis

- Explain data trends or summaries 

- Generate charts or visual summaries (with Excel/Power BI) 

- Assist with basic calculations or data interpretation 

# 3. Knowledge and Research

- Answer questions based on policies or public info 

Summarise legislation or guidance (non-legal) 

- Provide overviews of complex topics 

# 4. Idea Generation and Planning

- Brainstorm ideas for campaigns or strategies 

- Draft vision statements or objectives 

Structure presentations or briefings 

# 5.2 Prohibited Information Types

While Artificial Intelligence (AI) tools offer significant potential to enhance policing capabilities, their use must be carefully managed to protect sensitive information and uphold national security standards. Leicestershire Police permits the use of OFFICIAL and OFFICIAL-SENSITIVE information with approved AI tools, including personal data, provided the tool has undergone a formal risk assessment and has been explicitly authorised for use within the Force. 

However, due to the nature of policing and the critical importance of safeguarding sensitive operations, the following types of information must never be entered, pasted, uploaded, or otherwise submitted into Microsoft's Co-pilot or any other AI tool—regardless of the device or platform: 

- Any data marked or implicitly classified as SECRET or TOP SECRET. Any material classified at these levels, whether explicitly marked or implicitly understood, is strictly prohibited from AI ingestion. 

- Covert Human Intelligence Sources (CHIS) Related Information 

This includes any data that identifies or could potentially identify CHIS, their handlers, or associated covert operations. 

Information Subject to Specific Handling Conditions 

Each instance will require to be considered on a case by case basis. If you are unsure if a document may be ingested into an AI model, you must seek the express permission of the author BEFORE ingesting it into the model. Examples of handling conditions include, but is not limited to 

UK EYES ONLY (UKEO) 

Traffic Light Protocol - Red 

Data governed by international agreements with handling restrictions. 

Legally privileged information 

This includes communications protected under legal professional privilege, such as solicitor-client correspondence or court-prepared documentation. 

- Sensitive Operational Policing Data 

Avoid inputting highly sensitive operational details, such as specific tactics in active use, uncorroborated intelligence, or detailed profiles of ongoing covert operations. General discussion or summarisation of concluded, non-sensitive operational reports may be acceptable if other restrictions are met. Focus on business objectives and avoid inclusion of sensitive operational specifics. 

- System Credentials 

Passwords, API keys, private certificates, or any other system access credentials. Discussing general system configurations, non-critical vulnerabilities for analysis, or network architecture concepts is permitted if this is necessary for Leicestershire Police's digital services work. 

Guiding Principle: If you are unsure whether a piece of information is suitable for use in Copilot Chat, do not input it. Always steer on the side of caution. Seek the advice of your line manager or the Force's Information Security team first. 

# 5.3 What should I consider when using AI?

The use of AI and in policing is relatively new. It is complex technology that appears to have obvious and immediate benefits across a range of policing activities so it is likely that officers and staff of all ranks and roles will quickly experiment and adopt its use with well-founded good intentions. 

However, there are number of risks that are real, likely to occur and have the potential to have a high impact on individuals and public confidence in policing. Users are therefore expected to be cautious and professional. Although the below section will highlight several benefits and risk associated with the use of LLM's, there are a few simple steps that users can take to significantly reduce the risks of using generative AI. 

1. Only use AI tools that are authorised – (see Approved AI above) 

2. Always ensure you're using a force account on a force device - the use of personal accounts is prohibited. No policing data should ever be put into AI on a personal device or account. Only AI authorised by this policy can be used, when logged in using your police account on a work device. For example, using a personal mobile phone with an AI-powered transcription app to record and transcribe a meeting, interview, or operational briefing is strictly prohibited. Such actions risk unauthorised data exposure and breach of Force policy. 

3. Think before use - is the use of Al in this case, sensible, appropriate and ethical? 

3. Protect our data - keep personal information secure, and out of the AI unless absolutely necessary. 

4. Check the results – AI is not $100\%$ reliable and you must always read and verify anything produced. AI Models, Including LLM's, can sometimes 'hallucinate'—this means they may generate information that sounds plausible but is actually incorrect, made up, or not supported by real data. These hallucinations can include invented facts, inaccurate details, or even entirely fictional references. It's important to verify AI-generated content, especially when accuracy is critical 

5. Be transparent – Don't claim AI produced work as your own. Although there is no need to declare every use of AI such as for note taking, drafting work you heavily edit or for research, transparency is essential to maintain the public trust so always declare the use of AI in any formal report, creative content or when it is used in decision making. Use a note such as, "Microsoft Copilot supported the creation of this document". 

6. Human Review - AI must not be allowed to make operational decisions without human review. AI can be used as a tool, to present options and wider considerations. However, the responsibility and liability remain with the police decision maker. AI can never be used as a defence or rationale for a poor decision. 

6. Report any concerns - raise any concerns, mistakes or breaches as soon as possible with your supervisor or the Data Protection team. The sooner they know about issues the sooner they can mitigate the impact. 

7. Maintain Professional Standards - Use AI in a way that upholds the integrity and professionalism expected in the police service. 

8. Comply with the Force's wider Policies and Procedures - A non-comprehensive list includes: 

- Standards of Professional Behaviour - Police Officers 

- Standards of Professional Behaviour - Police Staff 

Information Management Policy 

Information Security Policy and Procedure. 

- Internet Access and Social Networking Procedure 

The National Code of Ethics 

The Management of Police Information (MoPI) framework 

- All relevant Data Protection legislation including the UK GDPR and the Data Protection Act 2018. 

9. Avoid Bias and Harm - Be alert to potential bias, stereotyping, or inappropriate content in Copilot outputs. Report any concerning outputs to your supervisor 

# 5.4 Al use in the Criminal Justice System

The use of AI technologies is not yet authorised for use in the Criminal Justice process, including for the creation of evidential materials. This means it must not be used in the preparation of witness statements, MG forms, or any other court ready evidential reports. 

However, AI may be used to support investigators in understanding evidence or assisting with the investigative process, provided it is not involved in the generation of any official evidential documentation. 

The only exception to this is when AI MG Forms Generation are undertaken as part of an approved force trial, in conjunction with the East Midlands CPS and using GoodSAM AI – under the strict operational procedures detailed in the trial operational order. 

Important note - Al generated content must be checked as carefully as if you wrote it yourself - the author is responsible for the final content. 

# 5.5 Key Risks of Using AI in Policing

While Artificial Intelligence (AI) offers significant potential to enhance policing operations, it also introduces a range of risks that must be carefully managed. The 

following outlines key areas of concern and the associated mitigations in place within Leicestershire Police: 

# - Bias and Stereotyping

AI systems may reflect or amplify societal biases present in their training data. This can result in unintended reinforcement of stereotypes related to gender, race, profession, or other protected characteristics. 

Mitigation: Approved AI tools undergo bias testing and ethical review, but it is acknowledged that not all harmful content can be fully prevented. 

# Overrepresentation or Underrepresentation

Certain groups may be disproportionately represented—or excluded—in Al-generated outputs. For example, legitimate content relating to LGBTQ+ topics may be filtered or misrepresented. 

Mitigation: Microsoft and other vendors apply classifiers and prompt engineering techniques to reduce this risk, but users must remain vigilant. 

# - Inappropriate or Offensive Content

AI may generate content that is offensive, controversial, or inappropriate, including text, images, or audio containing harmful symbols, language, or themes. 

Mitigation: Safeguards and content filters are in place, but some material may still bypass these controls. Users must report any concerning outputs immediately. 

# Information Reliability

AI tools can produce inaccurate, misleading, or fabricated information—commonly referred to as "hallucinations." This includes misrepresentation of facts, sources, or context. 

Mitigation: Users must verify all AI-generated content before acting on it, especially where operational or evidential decisions are involved. 

# Multilingual Performance

AI systems used within the Force are optimised for English. Performance in other languages may be less accurate or fluent, potentially affecting usability and interpretation. 

Mitigation: Where multilingual support is required, human review and translation should be used to validate outputs. 

# Audio Input Limitations

Voice-based AI tools may struggle with background noise, regional accents, or unclear speech, leading to transcription errors or misinterpretation. 

Mitigation: Use in controlled environments is recommended, and manual review of transcriptions is required. 

# - Internet Dependence

Most AI tools rely on a stable internet connection. Connectivity issues can disrupt access, reduce performance, or cause data loss. 

Mitigation: Users should ensure secure and reliable network access when using AI tools, and avoid using them in offline or unstable environments. 

# 5.6 Expectations when using AI

The use of AI tools must always take place in accordance with the Code of Ethics, Data Protection Laws and the force values. Specific guidance is provided to assist users in making sound decisions: 

# 1. Use Responsibly and Ethically

- Use AI in line with legislation and policy. 

- AI should support, not replace, professional judgment, especially in sensitive situations. 

# 2. Protect Confidentiality

- Do not input personal, sensitive, or operationally classified information into AI. 

- Follow all data handling and information security policies. 

- All outputs generated by AI tools used in official documentation must be stored in accordance with MOPI and Leicestershire Police's data retention policies. 

# 3. Be Transparent When Required

- Declare when AI has been used in formal reports, public documents, or decision-making. 

- Ensure all outputs are reviewed and clearly attributed. 

# 4. Verify and Validate Outputs

Always fact-check and assess AI-generated content. 

- Users are responsible for the accuracy and appropriateness of any content they use. 

# 5. Avoid Bias and Harm

- Be alert to potential bias, stereotyping, or inappropriate content in AI outputs. 

Report any concerning outputs to your supervisor. 

# 6. Stay Within intended Use Cases

- Use Copilot only for appropriate tasks (e.g., drafting, summarising, research support). 

- Do not use it for legal interpretation, risk assessments, or direct public communication without review. 

# 6. Roles and Responsibilities

# Governance

The governance procedure for all new AI ideas is outlined above. Consultation with key stakeholders should be sought before submission (IT, Information Management, Information Security etc). If the implementation of the new AI product comes with increased expenses, it will then go to the Force Executive Board for final sign off. 

# Artificial Intelligence Board

Responsible for commissioning new AI ideas and authorising new trials, and project progression into 'business as usual'. 

# Executive Board

Responsible for authorising any additional expenditure in relation to developing AI or New AI Products. 

# Digital Board

Monitoring the use of AI will be covered by the Digital Board. 

# Strategic Lead

Oversees the development, review, and communication of the AI policy. 

- Ensures policy compliance across the force and coordinates updates in response to legal, technological, or operational changes. 

- Liaises with national bodies and internal stakeholders to align with best practices. 

# Data Protection Officer (DPO)

- Provides guidance on data protection compliance in relation to AI use. 

Reviews reported incidents or breaches involving AI tools. 

Supports risk assessments and data protection impact assessments (DPIAs) where applicable. 

# Line Managers / Supervisors

- Ensure their teams understand and comply with the policy. 

- Act as the first point of contact for questions or concerns about AI use. 

- Escalate any misuse or incidents to the DPO or Strategic Lead. 

# IT and Digital Services Team

Manages access to authorised AI tools (e.g., Microsoft Copilot). 

- Ensures technical safeguards are in place to prevent unauthorised use. 

- Monitors system usage and supports secure integration of AI tools. 

# All Leicestershire Police Staff and Officers

- Use only authorised AI tools in accordance with this policy. 

- Avoid inputting prohibited or sensitive information into AI systems. 

- Verify AI-generated content before use and maintain transparency in formal outputs. 

Report any concerns, errors, or breaches promptly to their supervisor or the DPO. 

# - Training (Team Leicestershire Academy)

Develops and delivers training on responsible AI use. 

- Ensures staff are aware of the risks, benefits, and ethical considerations of generative AI.
